The Core Tension, Resolved

  You've identified that "ongoing MMO-like game" and "boardgame-like victory conditions" are structurally at odds, and the resolution is elegant: the
   game world is persistent and never ends, but players achieve milestones within it that the game recognises, commemorates, and responds to.

  This maps directly to how live MMOs actually work. In World of Warcraft, killing a raid boss doesn't end the game — it grants a title, unlocks new
  content, and the world continues. The "win" is the achievement, not the termination. Apply this to Nexus Dominion:

  - A player achieves Economic Dominance → they receive a badge ("Merchant Overlord of the Cygnus Reach"), the galaxy registers it, and the game
  procedurally generates a response — rivals form a cartel to undercut them, new trade routes open, a pirate faction emerges to raid their wealth
  - A player completes the Research path → they unlock a new tier of technology, triggering a galaxy-wide "tech arms race" arc
  - A player controls the Syndicate → this becomes visible to other empires at a certain threshold, triggering a "Take Down the Syndicate" coalition
  arc

  The scenario/mission pack model sits on top of this naturally: a scenario is a curated starting state with a specific achievement as its target
  ("Control 3 sectors within 50 turns" or "Survive the Syndicate's assassination attempt"). The base game has no such constraint — you play until you
   stop or until you've collected the achievements that satisfy you.

  This also resolves the Syndicate question neatly. The Syndicate is not a separate victory condition — it's a parallel axis of power with its own
  achievements. Controlling the Syndicate is a badge, not an endgame. But it meaningfully changes how you pursue the other goals (economic control
  through the black market, military power through contracted mercenaries, diplomatic leverage through blackmail). And if you achieve full Syndicate
  control while also holding galactic military supremacy, that combination badge is something special — a prestige achievement that the procedural
  engine treats as a major narrative event.

  Focus Group: Victory Conditions

  Simulated session. Six participants, one moderator.

  Participants:
  - Marcus — 4X veteran, 1,800 hours in Stellaris, 600 in Civ VI. Wants strategic depth and a clear sense of having "won."
  - Priya — Narrative/city-builder player. Loves Crusader Kings for the stories, bounces off Stellaris for complexity. Represents the aspirational
  new player.
  - Jake — Competitive optimizer. Speedruns games, calculates win conditions from turn 1, min-maxes everything.
  - Sofia — Tabletop RPG background. Plays Eve Online and Diplomacy. "Crusader Kings meets Eve Online" is her wheelhouse.
  - Alex — Systems designer. Thinks in feedback loops, anti-snowball mechanics, and balance constraints.
  - Dr. Chen — Narrative/UX designer. Thinks in player motivation, emotional payoffs, and Bartle taxonomy.

  ---
  Moderator: Let's start with the two Economic victory thresholds on the table. One says "60% of galactic wealth." The other says "1.5× the net worth
   of the nearest rival." Reactions?

  Marcus: "60% of galactic wealth" is brutal and I love it. That's a real dominance threshold. But in a 100-empire game, it might be unreachable. If
  the galaxy has 100 players all accumulating wealth, getting to 60% means you have more than the other 99 combined. That's either a 200-turn slog or
   it never happens.

  Jake: Exactly the problem. "1.5× the nearest rival" is achievable and skill-expressible — I can target it, I know when I'm on track, I know who I
  need to outgrow. The 60% version has no tactical clarity because I can't control what 99 other empires are doing with their economies.

  Priya: Can I say something? Both of those feel really abstract to me. "Net worth"? "Galactic wealth"? What does that look like? When I play Catan I
   know I have 10 victory points. I want to see something like "you control the three wealthiest trade hubs in the galaxy" — that's a story I can
  understand.

  Dr. Chen: Priya is identifying something important. The condition needs a narrative form, not just a numeric form. "1.5× net worth" is a number.
  "Galactic economic hegemon" is a title you've earned. Both are the same mechanic, but only one feels like an achievement. The badge framing solves
  this — the badge name IS the narrative.

  Sofia: In Eve Online, economic victory is never a single threshold — it's about controlling the flow. You control the trade hubs, you set the
  prices, you have informants in rival markets. I want economic victory to feel like I own the economy, not that I happened to accumulate the most
  credits. There should be economic coercion — I can embargo rivals, I can corner the ore market, I can destabilise their currency.

  Alex: This is where I'd push back on having one Economic victory condition. There are actually three different economic playstyles here:
  accumulation (be richer), control (own the infrastructure), and manipulation (move the market). If we want "economic" to feel like a legitimate
  rival to "military conquest," it needs that depth. Maybe the Economic achievement has tiers — "Trade Prince" for wealth ratio, "Market Overlord"
  for sector hub control, "Cartel Boss" for market manipulation — and any of the three earns it.

  Marcus: I like that. Military has the same problem by the way. "2× all opponents combined" is an arms race condition. But I might also want
  "Undefeated in 20 consecutive battles" as a military achievement. That's a skill expression, not just a resource dump.

  ---
  Moderator: Let's talk Diplomatic. Coalition controlling 50% of territory vs alliance with 75% of empires.

  Jake: 75% alliance with 100 empires means I need 75 bilateral agreements. That's a spreadsheet game. No thanks.

  Sofia: But that's exactly the game I want to play. Managing 75 relationships across 100 bots with different personalities, memories of past
  betrayals, and shifting loyalties? That IS the Crusader Kings experience. The problem isn't the number — it's whether the diplomacy system has
  enough texture to make those 75 relationships feel different from each other.

  Dr. Chen: There's a depth vs accessibility tradeoff here. Jake's "spreadsheet game" is Sofia's "emergent drama." The system needs to feel
  manageable. I'd suggest the meaningful Diplomatic achievement isn't about raw count of allies — it's about narrative weight. You didn't make 75
  agreements; you brokered the peace between the Warlord and the Merchant, you convinced the Tech Rush empire to share their research, you turned
  your most dangerous rival into a treaty partner. Those are achievements that feel earned.

  Priya: What does a coalition DO though? If I form this huge alliance, do we all win together? Do I win because I founded it?

  Alex: The coalition question is actually the anti-snowball mechanic from a different angle. Coalitions form naturally against leaders. The
  Diplomatic achievement should be about leading a coalition that succeeds in its objective — stopping the dominant empire, or collectively holding
  the galaxy stable. It's less "I have the most allies" and more "I organised the resistance and it worked." The founder of the coalition that
  brought down the galactic threat gets the badge.

  Marcus: That's more interesting. Conquest victory is about being the unstoppable force. Diplomatic victory is about being the person who stops the
  unstoppable force. They're mirror images. That's elegant.

  ---
  Moderator: Research victory — "Complete all 8 tech levels" or "Complete all 3 tech tree paths to capstone."

  Jake: Capstone abilities are the only thing that makes Research interesting. If the victory is just "fill the bar," it's boring. I want there to be
   a moment when I unlock the capstone and something changes — a new weapon comes online, my ships get a jump drive, my economy gets an automated
  production modifier. The Research victory should feel like a technological singularity event, not a graduation ceremony.

  Sofia: And other empires should respond to it. If I'm visibly racing up the tech tree, the military empires should be coming for me before I get
  there. The Research path should feel dangerous — I'm the most vulnerable military target in the mid-game, betting everything on reaching the
  endgame alive.

  Priya: I actually love this one. There's a clear progress bar. I can see how far I am. I know what I'm working toward. This is the most "game-y" of
   all the conditions in a way that I find comfortable.

  Dr. Chen: Which means it's also the most gameable. Jake will find the optimal tech path in 20 minutes. The interesting design question is whether
  the draft mechanic — choosing one doctrine, giving up alternatives — creates genuine tension in the Research path. If I can always find the optimal
   route, it's not a strategic victory, it's an execution victory.

  Alex: The research draft should have an adversarial element. Bots also draft. If a rival takes the doctrine I need, I have to pivot. That's where
  Research gets interesting — it's not "complete the path," it's "complete a viable path despite competition."

  ---
  Moderator: Military victory. Survival. Where do these land?

  Marcus: Military as currently defined — "2× power of all opponents combined" — is a late-game accumulation race with no drama. I'd rather see
  Military victory defined as a campaign of dominance: "Defeated 10 distinct empires in combat, never lost a sector to invasion." That's a story.
  That's a badge I want to hang on my wall.

  Jake: From an optimizer's view, I want Military victory to have a risk condition attached. If I go for Military, I'm committing to aggression. That
   means I'm making enemies. The anti-snowball coalition mechanic is what creates the risk — the closer I get to Military victory, the harder the
  galaxy fights back. The achievement should have a "survived the coalition attempt to stop me" element baked in.

  Sofia: Survival as a victory condition feels weird in an ongoing game. "Outlast everyone" when the game never ends is just... being the last person
   playing? Maybe Survival becomes "Endurance" — you've played for X turns, faced Y coalition attempts against you, and are still standing. It's a
  badge for longevity and resilience, not a terminal condition.

  Dr. Chen: Survival might be better framed as the fallback prestige for players who didn't achieve a focused victory but played a complete, long
  game. "You didn't build the biggest fleet or dominate the economy, but you're still here, and that's harder than it sounds." It rewards a different
   kind of skill — adaptability, reading threats, knowing when to retreat.

  Alex: Two competing design concerns. One: every victory path needs a risk counterbalance — the closer you get, the harder the game pushes back.
  Two: players should be able to pursue multiple paths simultaneously and "close out" the one they happen to hit first. Military might be ahead by
  turn 80 but Research is close by turn 100. The game should track all paths and let you organically achieve the one that comes first, rather than
  forcing a choice at game start.

  ---
  Synthesis from the Focus Group

  The participants converged on several shared positions:

  1. Every victory condition needs a narrative form. The badge name is the achievement. "You accumulated 1.5× net worth" becomes "Merchant Overlord
  of the Outer Reaches." The number is the trigger; the title is the reward.

  2. Every victory path needs a risk/risk-counterbalance. The closer you get to any achievement, the harder the galaxy pushes back. This is already
  the anti-snowball coalition mechanic. It needs to apply symmetrically to all six paths — not just Conquest.

  3. Multi-path tracking. Players should be able to pursue all six paths and achieve whichever comes first, rather than committing to one at game
  start. The strategic interest comes from the galaxy reading your trajectory and responding to it.

  4. Economic needs texture (accumulation, control, manipulation). A single wealth threshold is too shallow. Three economic sub-achievements ("Trade
  Prince," "Market Overlord," "Cartel Boss") give economic players real strategic variety.

  5. Military needs a campaign definition, not a static ratio. "Defeated in detail" (number of empires eliminated, sectors never lost) is more
  narratively satisfying than "highest number."

  6. Research needs adversarial drafting. The path isn't interesting unless rival bots are competing for the same doctrine slots.

  7. Survival becomes "Endurance" — a longevity prestige badge, not a terminal victory condition.

  8. Diplomatic victory is the mirror of Conquest. Conquest is being the unstoppable force. Diplomatic is organising the force that stops the
  unstoppable.

  What This Means for the Design Doc

  The architecture of the game is now clearer:

  - The galaxy is persistent. No turn limit, no time limit, no forced ending.
  - Achievements are the victory layer. They can be pursued simultaneously. The first one you hit is commemorated. You can keep playing and collect
  more.
  - Scenarios are curated challenge modes. They set a specific achievement as a target with constraints (time, turn count, resources). The base game
  has none of these constraints.
  - Procedural generation is the expansion engine. Each achievement triggers a galaxy response that generates new angles of play — new factions, new
  threats, new trade routes, new Syndicate contracts.
  - The Syndicate is a power axis, not a separate victory path. But "Shadow Throne" — controlling the Syndicate while holding another achievement —
  is the prestige badge for the most complex play.

  This gives us the skeleton for the full game design document. Want me to draft that now, starting from first boot and walking through the full
  player journey with these frameworks in place?
